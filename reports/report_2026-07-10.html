Broader SEC Cooperation Credit Framework — Implications for Public Companies

Reuters' recent coverage of the SEC's updated cooperation credit guidelines for organizations represents an important development for any public company currently managing undisclosed regulatory inquiries. The framework creates a tiered credit system: companies that voluntarily self-report receive the most favorable treatment; those who cooperate after being contacted but before a formal investigation receive intermediate credit; and those who cooperate only under compulsion receive minimal credit. Practically, this means boards of directors and audit committees at companies with known accounting anomalies, undisclosed related-party transactions, or FCPA exposure face a time-sensitive decision about self-reporting. The SEC's recent settlement activity — as analyzed by Skadden — suggests enforcement is concentrating in financial statement fraud, crypto asset disclosure failures, and cybersecurity incident disclosure compliance, all areas where cooperation credit could represent the difference between a manageable settlement and existential litigation.
